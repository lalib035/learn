Lesson 1: Audience Analysis



Audience analysis is the process of identifying the document's audience. It is one of the first steps in the process of technical writing.

A document's audience should shape how the document is written. Audience analysis determines the document's tone, level of technicality, and format.

Continue Lesson

Lesson Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • define an audience
  • investigate what readers ask about documents
  • analyse the communication context
  • gather audience information
  • profile an audience



Defining Audience

What is an audience? How would you define audience?

An audience is a group of readers who will read, use, and react to a specific piece of writing.

Every person has experienced being an audience member.

The writer must consider the following:

  • Who will read this document
  • How best to write for this audience
  • What to do if the document has more than one type of reader

Importance of Audience

Why identify your audience?

Most specialists agree that reader-centred writing distinguishes technical communication from other writing disciplines.

In a recent issue of the academic journal Technical Communication, the editor lists a key competency of technical communicators: "[e]mpathy with the user and an understanding of professional and organizational contexts."

This empathy and understanding allows technical communicators to create documents that best fit the audience's needs.

What questions do readers ask about a document?

  • What is it for?
  • Whom is it for?
  • What is being described or explained?
  • How do I [carry out the procedure]?
  • What will happen if I…?
  • What are the risks of…?



Context & Purpose

When analysing the communication's context, you should consider:

  • when the information will be used
  • how often and for how long will people be using the information
  • the reader's political/social situation or position in a work hierarchy
  • if gender is a factor in the document's use
  • if there are any legal issues surrounding the document's use

Is the purpose of your document to:

  • inform—e.g. manual, brochure, poster, email
  • persuade—e.g. brochure, poster, newsletter, web site
  • educate—e.g. tutorial, manual, web site
  • describe—e.g. manual, web site, online help
  • define—e.g. reference book, reference card
  • sell—e.g. brochure, poster, website

Reader Strategies

It is important to remember that readers adopt various reading strategies depending on what they want from a document.

Readers may be doing any of the following:

  • deciding whether to read
  • browsing
  • skimming
  • reading for gist
  • scanning (to find a particular piece of information)
  • reading in detail

Types of Readers

Expert: Specialised readers (academics/developers/engineers/practitioners)

For these readers, it is important to:

  • include technical facts and figures without long passages of background information
  • remember a document written for experts is only easily understood by experts

Semi-expert: Readers with a technical background, but not specialised (operators/technicians)

For these readers, it is important to:

  • write for the lowest level of understanding of the group
  • create non-technical documents suitable for readers with no specialised knowledge

Generalists and Non-Technical: Laypeople, skilled readers but educated in a different field

For these readers, it is important to:

  • minimise jargon
  • provide familiar comparisons
  • limit formulas/equations



Choosing Style

How do you decide on your writing style?

Consider that the reader is reading for a purpose. As the writer, you should focus on providing useful information for the reader.

If you are writing for more than one level of reader, you should first identify the primary readers. Include cues for readers from other audiences like references to further information, glossaries, definitions, and tables and graphs.

Consider:

  • level of detail/formality
  • phraseology
  • types of graphics

Audience Profiling

When gathering audience information, ask yourself the following:

  • What is my credibility as the originator?
  • Who is my audience?
  • What stated purpose, information, and language are necessary for my communication?
  • What means are necessary to transmit the message clearly?
  • What devices will make my message more understandable?
  • What reaction do I want?

Profiling an audience requires understanding who your audience is and what they need from the document.

Consider these reader characteristics when profiling your audience:

  • Reason for reading and attitude towards topic
  • Job title/responsibilities
  • Available time
  • Education and professional background
  • English-language ability
  • Reading context (physiological and physical)



General Strategies

No matter who your audience is, it is important that readers can find and focus on key information quickly.

You should:

  • state main points up front
  • use access devices—headings, table of contents, index, page numbers, formatting
  • use topic sentences
  • use lists
  • tell readers why information is relevant
  • explain how communication meets readers' needs
  • make the writing easy to read without dumbing it down
  • remove unnecessary words and obfuscations
  • use the active (not passive) voice

To further improve a finished document draft, you should:

  • interview users about their experience
  • provide opportunities for audience to give feedback directly
