Lesson 2: Writing Style



Writing style is the way writing is dressed up (or down) to fit the specific context, purpose, or audience. Word choice, sentence fluency, and the writer's voice all contribute to the style of a piece of writing.

Continue Lesson

Lesson Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

  • recognise writing styles and elements of style
  • make use of Strunk and White's elementary principles
  • apply John Kirkman's guidelines for good style



What is Writing Style

Style also depends on:

  • word choice
  • sentence construction
  • sentence length
  • transitions
  • tone

Professional writing style must be readable: clear, concise, fluent, and precise.

Poor style can lead to ineffective comprehension of text, reader frustration, and even a confused or misled reader.

Readable Style

It is the task of a technical writer to ensure that proper thought goes into writing style.

Some considerations:

  • The reader should be able to understand each sentence upon first reading
  • The maximum level of information should be expressed in the fewest words
  • Related ideas should be combined for fluency
  • Each word must have an exact meaning
  • The text should use concrete and specific language
  • The tone should be unbiased and inoffensive
  • The level of formality must be appropriate to the communication



Elementary Principles

The Elements of Style, by William Strunk and E.B. White, includes further suggestions for writing clear, direct English.

Elementary Principles of Composition:

  1. Choose a suitable design and hold to it
  2. Make the paragraph the unit of composition
  3. Use the active voice/avoid using the passive voice
  4. Word statements in a positive form
  5. Use definite, specific, concrete language
  6. Omit needless words and avoid needless repetition
  7. Avoid a succession of loose sentences and vary sentence length
  8. Express co-ordinate ideas in the same form (principle of parallelism)
  9. Keep related words together in a sentence
  10. In summaries, keep to one verb tense
  11. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end

Kirkman's Good Style

John Kirkman is a Professor of Communication Studies. In his book Good Style, he explains the tactics that can be used to write technical material in a coherent, readable style. The appropriateness of style choices depend on the document's audience, as discussed in Lesson 1: Audience Analysis.

The readers' context may include:

  • familiarity with subject
  • motivation
  • expectations about document (regarding content and style/organisation/layout)
  • attitude towards information or information publisher
  • readers' physical and mental state
  • physical appearance of text


Style Choices

Writers can adapt many aspects of style to the document's intended audience.

Stylistic choices include:

  • the length and complexity of sentences
  • the type of vocabulary used, be it long or short, familiar or unfamiliar, ordinary or grandiose, non-technical or technical
  • whether the verb forms are active or passive
  • whether or not to use paragraphing
  • the careful or casual use of punctuation
  • whether phrasing is comfortable or stiff, direct or roundabout

A number of these choices will be explained further.

Sentence Structure

Studies suggest that only a certain number of units of information can be stored in short-term memory. Wilson's 7 +/- 2 rule comes from a study that found that the average person can store 7 units of information, plus or minus two, in their short term memory. In practice, this means it is harder to remember the content of longer sentences.

Sentence structures which produce long sentences:

  • Compound sentences: long sentences formed by two or more main clauses
  • Complex sentences: sentences which include at least one main clause and at least one subordinate clause

Shorter sentences are more manageable. Don't overload any one sentence with more information than the reader can take. Science, business, and technical writing are littered with examples of long, unmanageable sentences.

While shorter sentences are usually easier to understand, clarity and readability are most important. Long sentences should be used when they are necessary to understand a concept.

Vocabulary & Verbs

Kirkman suggests writers should, where possible, use shorter and more familiar words. Using longer words, where a simpler word would work, can make you sound pretentious. However, longer words should only be replaced by shorter words with the same meaning.

Verb Tense

Be careful not to mix verb tenses thoughtlessly within a section of a document or in summaries. Inconsistent use of verb tenses can confuse readers and impact the document's professionalism.

Voice

The passive voice can change emphasis and meaning, add to the word count, and hide the performer of an action.

  • Active: "John designed the laboratory."
  • Passive: "The laboratory was designed (by John)."

While sometimes the passive voice can be necessary, you should limit its use to less than 10% of any document's sentences.
