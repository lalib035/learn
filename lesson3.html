Lesson 3: Summaries



Good technical writers communicate both clearly and concisely. Summaries are a common application of this straightforward writing style.

A summary is a brief, factual account of the content of a report or article.

Summaries often appear in documents such as:

  • reports and theses
  • websites and eLearning packages
  • government publications
  • journal articles

Because summaries can introduce or replace reading a document, it is important that readers find the summaries complete and easy to understand.

Lesson Objectives

Types of Summaries

There are two primary types of summaries: descriptive summaries and informative summaries.

Descriptive summaries give an overview of the topics covered by a report or article.

Informative summaries include more detail about the document and allow readers to decide whether or not to read the full document.

Descriptive Summaries

Descriptive summaries can be viewed as a document's table of contents in paragraph form.

These summaries generally list the topics discussed in the document but do not explain the conclusions of these discussions. Descriptive summaries are often included in electronic databases.

Effective descriptive summaries often include keywords so interested potential readers can quickly identify the document's contents.

Generally, descriptive summaries use objective language (avoid the use of 'I') and use the present tense. They may also refer to the document being summarized with an introductory phrase such as, "this article/thesis/web site examines/reports on/discusses..."



Descriptive Sample

Below is a sample descriptive summary. Read through the summary. See if you can identify at least five features of a descriptive summary in the sample.

"This report discusses a series of tests conducted on industrial-strength coil springs at the TopTech Laboratories in Northton, Minnesota in January 2008. It outlines the three kinds of springs evaluated and analyses their relative suitability for specific manufacturing applications. It concludes with a summary of the findings."

Descriptive summary features:

  • lists the topics
  • does not explain the conclusions
  • includes key words
  • uses objective language and present tense
  • may refer to original document

Informative Summaries

While descriptive summaries introduce the original document, good informative summaries replace the original document. Informative summaries thus include conclusions and recommendations, unlike descriptive summaries.

Informative summaries:

  • are written objectively
  • do not refer to the original document
  • avoid the use of "I"
  • include the essential message (conclusions/recommendations) of the original document
  • stand alone from the original document
  • do not include unessential examples or names of people or places
  • use a non-technical style



Informative Questions

Informative summaries help readers answer a number of questions.

These questions may include:

  • Which part of the subject area is this paper about?
  • Does the writer cover any special areas I am interested in?
  • What line does the writer take with the subject?
  • Is this a survey, or a report of new research?
  • Is this new to me or am I already familiar with the information?
  • Does the author come to clear-cut conclusions and how important are they to me?
  • Do I need to read this?

Informative Sample

Read through the sample informative summary. See if you can identify at least five features of an informative summary in the sample.

"Subjects' car clocks were set ten minutes fast in order to determine if deliberately setting a clock ahead will reduce lateness. One group of subjects knew their clocks had been set ahead, while a second group of subjects was not informed of the change. Over a four-week period, the subjects who were aware of the clock change regularly arrived on time or late for their scheduled appointments. Over the same period of time, the subjects who were unaware of the clock change tended to arrive early or on time for their appointments. Data suggest that intentionally setting a clock to run fast does not reduce lateness because one accounts for that extra time in his or her schedule."

Informative summaries:

  • are written objectively and avoid the use of "I"
  • stand alone from and do not refer to the original document
  • include the essential message (conclusions/recommendations) of the original document
  • do not include unessential examples or names of people or places
  • use a non-technical style

Revision Checklist

When editing or revising a summary, it can be useful to ask the following questions.

  • Does the summary contain only the essential message?
  • Can the summary stand alone?
  • Is the summary accurate?
  • Is the summary free of additions to the original?
  • Is the summary free of needless details?
  • Is the summary brief yet clear and comprehensive?
  • Is the level of technical detail appropriate for the audience?
